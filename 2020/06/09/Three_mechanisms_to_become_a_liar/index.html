Three mechanisms to become a good liar

發表於 2020-06-09   |   分類於 blog

To be honest, this article is not about how to tell lies as its title; instead, it can help you identify lies. These days, I have been learning how to present and visualize data via various charts, namely, bar chart, line graph and scatter plot. Before I am exposed to many technical details, I knew a fact first which is a significant number of statical charts are incorrect, fake and misleading. You can see this kind of charts, graphs in different places including news, websites, TV, books and even papers. “However, the mirror image of that awareness, the study of how graphs, charts, maps, and diagrams can be used to deceive, has remained within the boundaries of academic circles in statistics, cartography, and computer science.”

There are endless reasons why people make misleading charts, however, I don’t want to explore these reasons. I just want to show three mechanisms that they normally use to mislead the public. And what’s more important, I really want to improve your awareness about this problem and introduce something about critical thinking. If you want to know more about critical thinking, you can read the book which is called Asking the Right Questions: A Guide to Critical Thinking.

Here are three strategies which are used to deceive: Hiding relevant data to highlight what benefits us, Displaying too much data to obscure reality, Using graphic forms in inappropriate ways (distorting the data). I will show some examples to explain these three mechanisms.

He proudly holds the average sales increase of his company over the last year: 12 %. It is good, isn’t it? Let’s look at the second picture. The fact is that among the ten markets in which this company operates, ﬁve improved, but the other ﬁve shrank.


The most common one is distorting the data. The next picture is the results of the presidential election results in Venezuela. Notice the truncated Y-axis which greatly distorts the difference between the percentages of the vote, and this is because Y-axis does not begin with zero.

If we add a zero-baseline, it will be the next picture. If we add a zero-baseline, it will be the next picture. You can see the difference is extremely small.

Here is another example. We can see from this picture that x-axis is not starting from zero, instead, it begins with 0.55%. This way exaggerates 0.1%–a very small value. This article uses blue and red colour to show the figure which can attract readers’ attention easily, and readers may only notice the ‘big’ gap between this two-colour. What’s more, the font of the figures is very small, because of this, readers are more likely to pay little attention to the figure.
